Constitutional Rights Basics

What are Fundamental Rights in India?

Fundamental Rights are the basic human rights enshrined in the Constitution of India which are guaranteed to all citizens. They are enforceable by the courts, subject to specific restrictions. The six fundamental rights include:

  • Right to Equality (Articles 14-18)
  • Right to Freedom (Articles 19-22)
  • Right against Exploitation (Articles 23-24)
  • Right to Freedom of Religion (Articles 25-28)
  • Cultural and Educational Rights (Articles 29-30)
  • Right to Constitutional Remedies (Article 32)

Can fundamental rights be suspended?

Yes, fundamental rights can be suspended during a state of emergency. Under Article 352 of the Indian Constitution, when a national emergency is declared, certain fundamental rights under Article 19 can be automatically suspended. However, rights under Articles 20 and 21 (protection in respect of conviction for offenses and right to life and personal liberty) cannot be suspended even during an emergency.

What is a writ petition?

A writ petition is a formal written application made to a court exercising extraordinary jurisdiction. In India, under Article 32 and Article 226 of the Constitution, the Supreme Court and High Courts respectively can issue writs for enforcement of fundamental rights. The five types of writs are:

  • Habeas Corpus: To produce a person who has been detained
  • Mandamus: To command a public authority to perform its duty
  • Prohibition: To prohibit a lower court from proceeding with a case
  • Certiorari: To transfer a case from a lower court to a higher court
  • Quo Warranto: To question the legal right of a person holding a public office

What is the difference between Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles?

The key differences between Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles of State Policy are:

  • Enforceability: Fundamental Rights are enforceable by courts, while Directive Principles are not enforceable by any court.
  • Nature: Fundamental Rights are mostly negative in nature (prohibit the state from doing something), while Directive Principles are positive (direct the state to secure certain objectives).
  • Aim: Fundamental Rights aim to establish political democracy, while Directive Principles aim to establish social and economic democracy.
  • Priority: In case of conflict, Fundamental Rights generally prevail over Directive Principles, though the Constitution has been amended to give priority to certain Directive Principles.

Legal Help

How can I find a lawyer through this service?

While our Constitutional Rights Assistant does not directly connect you with lawyers, it can provide information on how to find legal representation:

  • Ask the assistant about legal aid services in your area
  • Request information about your state's legal services authority
  • Get guidance on how to approach district legal services authorities
  • Learn about pro bono legal services for economically disadvantaged citizens

You can also ask for information about bar associations in your region, which can help you find qualified lawyers specializing in your specific legal issue.

What is legal aid and am I eligible?

Legal aid refers to the provision of assistance to people who are unable to afford legal representation and access to the court system. In India, legal aid is a fundamental right under Article 39A of the Constitution.

You may be eligible for free legal aid if you belong to any of these categories:

  • Members of Scheduled Castes or Scheduled Tribes
  • Victims of human trafficking or beggar
  • Women and children
  • Persons with disabilities
  • Victims of mass disaster, ethnic violence, caste atrocity, flood, drought, earthquake, or industrial disaster
  • Industrial workmen
  • Persons in custody
  • Persons with annual income below the specified limit (varies by state)

You can contact your State Legal Services Authority or District Legal Services Authority to apply for legal aid.

How do I file a Public Interest Litigation (PIL)?

Public Interest Litigation (PIL) is a petition that can be filed by any public-spirited person in the High Court or Supreme Court for the protection of public interest. To file a PIL:

  1. Identify the issue: The matter should concern public interest, affecting the rights of a community or class of people, not just an individual grievance
  2. Research: Gather all relevant facts, documents, and evidence related to the issue
  3. Draft the petition: Prepare a petition clearly stating the facts, legal provisions violated, and the remedy sought
  4. Filing: Submit the petition to the appropriate High Court or the Supreme Court along with required affidavits and documents
  5. Court fees: Pay the nominal court fee (much lower than regular litigation)

PILs can be filed through a simple letter addressed to the Chief Justice of the High Court or Supreme Court in some cases, especially if the petitioner cannot afford formal filing procedures.

What should I do if my rights have been violated?

If you believe your constitutional or legal rights have been violated, you can take the following steps:

  1. Document everything: Collect and preserve all evidence related to the violation
  2. File a police complaint: For criminal violations, file an FIR at the local police station
  3. Approach relevant authorities: Contact appropriate government departments or regulatory bodies
  4. Seek legal counsel: Consult a lawyer who specializes in the relevant area of law
  5. File a case: Depending on the nature of violation, file a case in the appropriate court
  6. Approach commissions: Contact specialized commissions like the Human Rights Commission, Women's Commission, SC/ST Commission, etc.
  7. Writ petition: File a writ petition in the High Court or Supreme Court for fundamental rights violations

Our Constitutional Rights Assistant can provide more specific guidance based on the nature of the rights violation you have experienced.
